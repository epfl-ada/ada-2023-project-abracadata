A little about the weather dataset

The United States is geographically huge – the weather can differ from state to state, or even city to city! This brings up the question: how do we generalize the weather to accurately represent the different patterns across the United States?

Our solution to this question was to figure out which cities represented the US best in terms of population and movie theater concentration, while still ensuring we captured a proper representation of all weather types present. This gave us the following 8 cities: New York, Los Angeles, Chicago, Seattle, Washington DC, Atlanta, Miami, and Dallas (not ranked in terms of how much we actually like them).

For those who haven't visited all eight cities before, here's a comparison of each of the eight cities and their distributions in terms of precipitation and temperature. Notice how, as expected, Miami is consistently very warm whereas cities like Chicago or New York have a wider distribution and more bad weather days (snow days and cold days).

What about Natural Disasters?

One thing that we did not take note of was natural disasters. Similar to Iceland's volcanoes, many of these parts of the US have some sort of natural disaster. In Miami and Atlanta, these two cities often experience hurricanes every year. When hurricanes hit, all people in the cities nearest to the southeast coast are forced to evacuate for at least one week. I'm not sure how many people think of going to the theaters when their house is flooded with muddy water, but this definitely can affect sales, especially if the opening weekend falls at the same time as a hurricane landing! Not in the list of cities we analyzed but still a fairly important city, San Francisco also has its fair share of natural disasters: earthquakes. These don't happen nearly as often as hurricanes, but if a big earthquake does hit, it can affect a large portion of the area around the epicenter, leading to a definite decrease in movie attendance.

Our Redefinition of "Average" Temperature

The average temperature calculations are typically calculated using a formula very similar to \(T_{\mathrm{avg}} = \frac12 \cdot T_{\mathrm{high}} + \frac12 \cdot T_{\mathrm{low}}\). However, this assumes that people are active at all times of the day, meaning it will take into consideration days where the morning may be particularly cold but the during the day, the temperature warms up significantly.

To mitigate this effect, we checked a few daily temperature graphs for the cities and found that the trend in daily temperature is roughly the same throughout the day: the coldest time is around 6 AM and the warmest is around 3 PM. We isolated the portion of the graph to when people are most likely to be outside watching movies (highlighted in blue on the graph), especially given movie showtimes.

Given this graph, we can see that a better estimate of the average daily temperature for the purposes of our data analysis would be \(T_{\mathrm{avg}} = \frac23 \cdot T_{\mathrm{high}} + \frac13 \cdot T_{\mathrm{low}}\). This accurately captures how people generally choose afternoon or evening, both warmer times of the day, to watch movies.

What exactly is "good" and "bad" weather?

The definition of "good" and "bad" weather is hghly subjective, but we decided on definitions based on a group consensus. Our initial naive definition of "bad" weather is as follows:

  1. The average temperature was colder than usual -- you might want to stay home on an especially cold winter day
  2. There is more rain or snow than usual -- we prefer not getting our clothes wet most of the times
